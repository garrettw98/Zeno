## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Ideas don't become law by themselves. The Human Standard's policies must navigate the complex American legislative system—federal and state, executive and legislative, constitutional and political. This document provides a realistic roadmap for turning philosophy into policy.

**The Core Insight:** Understanding the system's constraints is not cynicism—it's strategy. We plan for the system we have, not the one we wish we had.

## PART I: THE LEGISLATIVE LANDSCAPE

### 1.1 Federal Legislative Process

**Constitutional Requirements:**

1. **Bill Introduction:** Member of House or Senate introduces bill
2. **Committee Referral:** Bill assigned to relevant committee(s)
3. **Committee Action:** Hearings, markup, vote to report
4. **Floor Action:** Debate, amendment, vote
5. **Other Chamber:** Repeat process in House or Senate
6. **Conference:** Resolve differences between chambers
7. **Presidential Action:** Sign, veto, or pocket veto
8. **Veto Override:** 2/3 of both chambers (if vetoed)

**Time Required:**
- Simple bills: 6 months - 2 years
- Complex legislation: 2-5 years
- Transformative reform: 5-15 years

### 1.2 The Filibuster Reality

**Current Rules (Senate):**
- 60 votes needed to end debate (cloture)
- Effectively requires 60 votes to pass most legislation
- Budget reconciliation requires only 51 votes (but limited scope)

**Implications for Human Standard:**
- Citizen's Royalty funding mechanism → possibly through reconciliation
- AI governance → needs 60 votes (regulatory)
- Tax restructuring → possibly through reconciliation
- Constitutional amendments → 67 votes Senate, 290 votes House, 38 states

### 1.3 Budget Reconciliation Path

**What Can Pass with 51 Votes:**
- Tax changes (automation tax, data tax, VAT)
- Spending changes (Citizen's Royalty funding)
- Deficit impact provisions

**What Cannot Pass via Reconciliation:**
- Regulatory changes (AI governance)
- Social Security modifications (separate rules)
- Non-budgetary policy (blockchain infrastructure)

**Strategy:** Split Human Standard into:
- **Budget Package:** Tax and spending (reconciliation)
- **Regulatory Package:** AI governance (60 votes or separate)

### 2.2 Constitutional Basis for Each Policy

#### Citizen's Royalty (Income Floor)
**Authority:** Article I, Section 8 (Spending Power)

**Precedent:**
- Social Security (1935) upheld in Helvering v. Davis
- Medicare/Medicaid (1965) unchallenged
- Tax credits (EITC) established practice

**Constitutional Argument:**
- Congress can spend for "general welfare"
- Citizen's Royalty is spending program, not mandate
- No constitutional barrier to direct payments

**Risk Level:** LOW - Strong constitutional foundation

#### Automation Productivity Tax
**Authority:** Article I, Section 8 (Taxing Power); 16th Amendment (Income Tax)

**Precedent:**
- Corporate income tax established
- Payroll taxes established
- Excise taxes broadly upheld

**Constitutional Argument:**
- Tax on business productivity is income/excise hybrid
- No constitutional prohibition on taxing automation
- Apportionment requirement satisfied if structured as income tax

**Risk Level:** LOW-MEDIUM - Depends on structure

#### AI Governance (Tiered Transparency)
**Authority:** Commerce Clause; Government Contracts; Administrative Procedure Act

**Precedent:**
- Government can set standards for contractors
- Regulatory requirements upheld for commercial activities
- Open records laws establish transparency precedent

**Constitutional Argument:**
- Apply to federal contractors and recipients of federal funds
- Commerce clause covers AI systems in interstate commerce
- First Amendment concerns minimal (code isn't protected speech in commercial context)

**Risk Level:** MEDIUM - Some litigation likely

#### Data Dividend Tax
**Authority:** Article I, Section 8 (Taxing Power); Commerce Clause

**Precedent:**
- Digital services taxes in other countries
- Internet taxation settled by South Dakota v. Wayfair (2018)
- No constitutional barrier to taxing digital services

**Constitutional Argument:**
- Tax on revenue is clearly constitutional
- Nexus established by user location
- Similar to advertising taxes

**Risk Level:** LOW-MEDIUM - Industry opposition, not constitutional issue

#### Wealth Tax
**Authority:** Disputed - 16th Amendment; Article I Direct Tax Clause

**Constitutional Argument (For):**
- 16th Amendment allows income tax without apportionment
- Mark-to-market treats appreciation as income
- Alternatively: Apportioned among states

**Constitutional Argument (Against):**
- Wealth is not income
- Direct tax requires apportionment
- Pollock v. Farmers' Loan & Trust (1895) precedent

**Risk Level:** HIGH - Supreme Court case likely, outcome uncertain

**Mitigation:**
- Structure as mark-to-market income tax
- Apply only to liquid assets (easier to value)
- Prepare alternative revenue sources if struck down

### 2.3 What Requires Constitutional Amendment?

**Definitely Requires Amendment:**
- Right to basic income (Economic Security Amendment)
- AI rights/protections (if extending to non-humans)
- Changing Senate representation or Electoral College

**Possibly Requires Amendment:**
- Wealth tax (depending on structure and court composition)
- Fundamental restructuring of federal power
- Binding future Congresses to Citizen's Royalty commitment

**Amendment Process:**
1. 2/3 vote in both House and Senate, OR
2. Constitutional convention called by 2/3 of state legislatures
3. Ratification by 3/4 of state legislatures (38 states)

**Timeline:** 5-15 years minimum (typically decades)

## PART III: LEGISLATIVE STRATEGY BY PACKAGE

### 3.1 Package A: The Foundation (Year 1-2)

**Contents:**
- Automation Productivity Tax (disclosure phase)
- Data Dividend Tax (initial implementation)
- Citizen's Royalty pilot program ($500/month, limited)
- NAICO establishment (AI governance office)
- Automation Observatory (monitoring)

**Legislative Vehicle:** Budget Reconciliation + Stand-Alone Bills

**Political Strategy:**
- Tie to deficit reduction (revenue positive)
- Frame as "modernizing tax code for AI age"
- Include benefits for small businesses
- Pilot program limits political risk

**Vote Requirements:**
- Budget provisions: 51 Senate, 218 House
- NAICO establishment: 60 Senate (filibuster), 218 House

**Likely Opposition:**
- Tech industry (data tax)
- Business groups (automation tax disclosure)
- Fiscal conservatives (new spending)

**Counter-Strategy:**
- Show revenue neutrality
- Emphasize small business exemptions
- Highlight pilot approach (not permanent commitment)

### 3.2 Package B: The Expansion (Year 3-4)

**Contents:**
- Automation Productivity Tax (5% rate)
- Carbon tax ($50/ton)
- Citizen's Royalty Phase 2 ($18k floor)
- AI governance regulations
- Blockchain infrastructure pilots

**Legislative Vehicle:** Budget Reconciliation + Regulatory Action

**Political Strategy:**
- Point to pilot program success
- Build on NAICO establishment
- Use regulatory authority where possible
- Executive orders for federal systems

**Vote Requirements:**
- Tax/spending: 51 Senate (reconciliation)
- Regulations: Can be done administratively
- Some provisions may need 60 votes

**Likely Opposition:**
- Fossil fuel industry (carbon tax)
- Expanding business opposition
- Skeptics of pilot results

**Counter-Strategy:**
- Show pilot program outcomes
- Build coalition with climate advocates
- Demonstrate public support

### 3.3 Package C: Full Implementation (Year 5-7)

**Contents:**
- Automation Tax at full rate (15%)
- Data tax at full rate
- Citizen's Royalty at full amount (the Citizen's Royalty (up to $12,000/year under the NIT model))
- Comprehensive AI governance
- Wealth tax (if constitutional)
- Program consolidation

**Legislative Vehicle:** Omnibus Bill + Budget Reconciliation

**Political Strategy:**
- Campaign on full vision in midterm/presidential elections
- Demonstrate momentum from early packages
- Build unstoppable political coalition
- Constitutional amendment process begins

**Vote Requirements:**
- Full implementation needs sustained majorities
- Wealth tax may need amendment
- Constitutional amendment for permanence

**Likely Opposition:**
- Entrenched interests fighting full implementation
- Constitutional challenges to wealth tax
- Federalism concerns

**Counter-Strategy:**
- Electoral mandate from winning elections
- Prepare alternative revenue if wealth tax fails
- State-by-state implementation where federal blocked

## PART IV: EXECUTIVE BRANCH STRATEGIES

### 4.1 Executive Orders (Day One)

**What Can Be Done Immediately:**

1. **Establish NAICO** (Executive Office reorganization)
   - Create National AI Coordination Office
   - Appoint leadership
   - Begin coordination role

2. **Federal AI Transparency** (Procurement policy)
   - Require AI transparency for federal contractors
   - Tiered transparency for new federal AI systems
   - Audit requirements for existing systems

3. **Automation Impact Assessment** (NEPA-style)
   - Require automation impact statements for federal projects
   - Establish review process
   - Create public database

4. **Diversified Strategic Reserve** (Treasury authority)
   - Begin acquisition program
   - Establish custody protocols
   - Transparency reporting

5. **Data Dignity Principles** (Federal agencies)
   - Data minimization requirements
   - Consent standards
   - Privacy by default

**Limitations:**
- Executive orders can be reversed by next president
- Cannot create new spending programs
- Cannot change tax law
- Subject to legal challenge

### 4.2 Regulatory Action

**Administrative Agencies Can:**

- Issue rules implementing existing statutory authority
- Require disclosures for regulated entities
- Condition permits/licenses on AI standards
- Enforce existing laws against AI-related harms

**Process:**
1. Notice of Proposed Rulemaking (NPRM)
2. Public comment period (60-90 days)
3. Final rule publication
4. Effective date (usually 30-60 days)
5. Judicial review available

**Timeline:** 6 months - 2 years per major rule

**Key Agencies:**
- FTC (data practices, AI fairness)
- SEC (AI in financial services)
- EEOC (AI in employment)
- DOT (autonomous vehicles)
- HHS (AI in healthcare)
- DOL (automation impact on workers)

### 4.3 Federal Contracts and Grants

**Powerful Leverage:**
- Federal government spends ~$7 trillion annually
- ~$700 billion in contracts
- ~$1 trillion in grants

**Conditions We Can Attach:**
- AI transparency requirements for contractors
- Open source requirements for federally-funded AI
- Data protection standards
- Worker impact assessments

**Precedent:**
- Davis-Bacon Act (wage requirements)
- Executive Order 11246 (affirmative action)
- Federal Acquisition Regulations (FAR)

## PART VI: THE AMENDMENT PATH

### 6.1 Economic Security Amendment

**Proposed Text:**

> Section 1. Every adult citizen of the United States shall have the right to a basic income sufficient to meet fundamental needs.
>
> Section 2. Congress shall have power to enforce this article by appropriate legislation, including the establishment of funding mechanisms and payment systems.
>
> Section 3. This right shall not be diminished by any subsequent legislation except by a two-thirds vote of both Houses of Congress.

**Ratification Strategy:**

**Phase 1: Build Momentum (Years 1-5)**
- Win legislative victories that demonstrate support
- Build state-level movements
- Engage civil society organizations
- Shift public opinion

**Phase 2: Congressional Action (Years 5-8)**
- Introduce amendment in Congress
- Build toward 2/3 support
- Alternative: Convention of States route

**Phase 3: Ratification (Years 8-15)**
- State-by-state campaign
- Target 38 states
- Build coalitions in red, blue, and purple states

**Historical Precedent:**
- 26th Amendment (18-year-old vote): 3 months from Congress to ratification
- 19th Amendment (women's suffrage): 14 months
- Most amendments: 1-4 years after Congress acts

### 6.2 AI Rights Amendment (Long-Term)

**Proposed Text:**

> Section 1. No person shall be subject to decisions affecting fundamental rights made solely by artificial intelligence without opportunity for human review.
>
> Section 2. Every person has the right to an explanation of automated decisions affecting their rights or welfare.
>
> Section 3. Congress and the States shall have power to enforce this article by appropriate legislation.

**Timeline:** 10-20 years (longer-term goal)
